## 01 Background

- Generative AI entered education and workplaces rapidly after 2022.
- China expected 12.22 million college graduates in 2025.
- STEM skills traditionally receive higher wage returns.
- Social science work is increasingly AI-assisted.

### Why This Question Matters

- Graduate labor-market competition is intensifying.
- AI may change the value of majors and workplace skills.
- The wage gap reflects broader skill-based inequality.
- The answer helps students prepare for AI-shaped work.

### Key Concepts

- **STEM graduates:** Science, technology, engineering, and mathematics majors.
- **Social science graduates:** Economics, management, law, sociology, communication, public administration, and related fields.
- **Wage differential:** The difference in average earnings between two graduate groups.

**Key tension:** Complement to STEM, or a general-purpose tool across majors?

## 02 Research Question(s)

**Main question:** Has generative AI widened or narrowed the wage gap between STEM and social science graduates in China?

1. Do technology-intensive occupations offer higher wages?
2. Are STEM majors still concentrated in high-paying early-career jobs?
3. Can social science graduates gain from AI-enhanced productivity?

### Research Logic

Generative AI -> complements STEM skills -> substitutes routine tasks -> spreads AI literacy across majors -> changes the wage gap.

- **Skill complementarity:** AI raises productivity for programming, data, and engineering skills.
- **Task substitution:** AI automates routine writing, analysis, coding, and administrative tasks.
- **Skill diffusion:** AI lets non-STEM graduates perform analytical and technical tasks more efficiently.

**H1:** AI complements technical skills, so the STEM premium may increase.  
**H2:** AI lowers barriers to analytical work, so the major-based gap may decrease.

**Expected answer:** AI is more likely to reshape the gap than move it in only one direction.

## 03 Method

### Data Analysis Design

This poster uses a descriptive and comparative design, not a full causal model.

1. Describe the technical-worker wage premium.
2. Compare early-career wages across majors.
3. Identify AI-skill demand in job postings.
4. Interpret whether AI widens, narrows, or reshapes the gap.

### Variable Map

| Dependent Variable | Independent Variables | Control Factors |
|---|---|---|
| Monthly wage | Major type | Region and industry |
| Annual wage | AI exposure | Occupation and experience |
| Advertised salary | AI skill requirements | Education level |
| STEM wage premium | Post-2022 AI diffusion | Employer type |

### Evidence Base

Official statistics; graduate employment reports; CGSS, CFPS, and CHFS; online job-posting studies.

### Interpretation Model

`log(Wage) = beta0 + beta1 STEM + beta2 PostAI + beta3 STEM x PostAI + Controls + epsilon`

`beta3` indicates whether the STEM premium changed after AI diffusion.

## 04 Results

### Graduate Labor Supply

**12.22 million graduates expected in China in 2025.**

**Interpretation:** A larger graduate population increases entry-level competition. Skills, majors, and AI literacy may matter more for wages.

### 2024 Wage Comparison

- Urban non-private sector: CNY 124,110 per year
- Urban private sector: CNY 69,476 per year
- Professional and technical workers: CNY 148,046 per year

**Interpretation:** Professional and technical workers earn above both urban-sector averages, showing that technical expertise remains strongly rewarded.

### AI Skill Demand

- Current ChatGPT-related skill demand: 28%
- Potential future demand: 45%

**Interpretation:** AI demand is not limited to programmers. As ChatGPT-related skills spread, social science graduates may gain if they develop AI literacy.

### High-Paying Undergraduate Majors

- Information Security: CNY 7,599 per month
- Microelectronics: CNY 7,282
- Electronic Science: CNY 7,215
- Automation: CNY 7,108
- Software Engineering: CNY 7,092
- Undergraduate average: CNY 6,199

**Interpretation:** The highest-paying majors are concentrated in information technology and engineering, supporting a continuing early-career STEM wage advantage.

### Evidence Summary

1. Technical workers earn above-average wages. **Implication:** STEM skills still have strong market value.
2. High-paying majors are mostly STEM-related. **Implication:** STEM may retain an early-career advantage.
3. AI skills are spreading across occupations. **Implication:** Social science graduates can also benefit.

**Overall implication:** Generative AI is more likely to reshape the wage gap than simply widen or narrow it.

## 05 Discussion & Conclusion

### Three Possible Outcomes

- **Widening gap:** AI mainly complements technical skills, increasing the STEM wage premium.
- **Narrowing gap:** AI lowers barriers to analysis, writing, and business research.
- **Reshaped gap:** Hybrid AI literacy plus domain knowledge becomes the main advantage.

### Final Conclusion

The evidence does not support a simple “STEM wins, social science loses” story. AI changes the structure of skill demand: STEM gains from technical complementarity, while social science can gain through AI-assisted analysis, communication, and decision-making.
